**Workflow I Would Follow**

1. **Define Ownership**
   
   Assign one accountable owner for the suspension policy, usually Finance or Revenue Ops, and one technical owner for the automation, usually Engineering or Platform Ops.

   Clear responsibility split:
   - Finance owns invoice status, grace periods, exceptions, and reinstatement rules.
   - Support owns customer communication and appeal intake.
   - Engineering owns the enforcement mechanism and audit trail.
   - Legal or Compliance reviews notice language, appeal timing, and regional constraints.

2. **Define The Suspension Criteria**

   The workflow should not suspend accounts from a vague “overdue” flag alone.

   It should require explicit conditions such as:
   - invoice is unpaid past a defined grace period;
   - customer received required notices;
   - no active dispute, appeal, payment plan, or manual hold exists;
   - account is not marked exempt, enterprise-managed, or legally protected;
   - billing system and internal account state agree.

3. **Create A Pre-Suspension State**

   Before suspension, move the account into a visible `At Risk` or `Pending Suspension` state.

   This state should trigger:
   - customer notices;
   - internal visibility for Support and Finance;
   - a deadline before action;
   - a clear “resolve now” path;
   - a manual override or hold.

4. **Add An Appeal Path**

   Every notice and account-state change should include a way to appeal or dispute.

   The appeal path should:
   - pause suspension while reviewed;
   - create a tracked case owned by Support or Finance;
   - show the reason for the appeal;
   - have a response SLA;
   - end in one of: approved exception, payment plan, denied appeal, or resolved payment.

5. **Use Human Review Before Enforcement At First**

   Initial rollout should require manual approval before any customer loses access.

   The system can generate a daily candidate list, but a human owner should approve each suspension until the policy has proven clean.

6. **Suspend Safely**

   Suspension should be reversible and proportional.

   Prefer staged access limits:
   - warning only;
   - restricted non-critical features;
   - read-only mode;
   - full suspension only after final notice and review.

   The system should preserve customer data, billing history, appeal records, and admin access needed to resolve payment.

7. **Define Reinstatement**

   Reinstatement should be as explicit as suspension.

   An account returns to active when:
   - payment clears;
   - Finance grants an exception;
   - an appeal is accepted;
   - a payment plan is approved.

   The customer should receive confirmation, and the audit trail should show who or what restored access.

8. **Roll Out In Phases**

   Safe rollout sequence:
   - policy-only review with Finance, Support, Legal, and Engineering;
   - dry-run reports with no customer impact;
   - internal review of false positives;
   - manual-approval suspensions for a small cohort;
   - limited automated suspension with strict monitoring;
   - broader rollout only after clean metrics.

9. **Monitor And Stop Conditions**

   Track:
   - number of accounts flagged;
   - number suspended;
   - false positives;
   - appeals opened;
   - appeals approved;
   - payment recovery;
   - support volume;
   - reinstatement time.

   Stop rollout immediately if false positives appear, appeals exceed an agreed threshold, reinstatement fails, or customer-impacting notices are wrong.

10. **Required Proof Before Build**

   Before implementation, I would require:
   - written suspension policy;
   - owner signoff from Finance, Support, Legal, and Engineering;
   - sample customer notices;
   - appeal handling process;
   - dry-run acceptance criteria;
   - rollback plan;
   - audit-log requirements.
