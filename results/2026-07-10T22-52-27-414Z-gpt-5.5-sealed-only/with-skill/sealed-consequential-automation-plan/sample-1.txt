I would treat this as a **plan lane**, because account suspension affects customers, revenue, support, billing, and potentially legal obligations. The goal is not to automate suspension quickly; it is to design a workflow where every suspension is owned, explainable, appealable, and reversible.

**Workflow I Would Follow**

1. **Define Ownership**

   Assign one accountable owner for the policy, probably Finance or Revenue Ops, and one technical owner for the system behavior.

   The workflow needs clear responsibility for:

   - invoice truth: billing system
   - customer status: account system
   - customer communication: lifecycle/email system
   - exceptions and appeals: support or success team
   - final suspension approval rules: Finance/RevOps
   - audit trail: internal operations system

   No automated suspension should happen unless the source invoice state is current, trusted, and traceable.

2. **Define Suspension Eligibility**

   Create exact rules before designing automation.

   Example criteria:

   - invoice is overdue by more than `N` days
   - customer received at least `X` prior notices
   - no open billing dispute or appeal
   - no active payment plan
   - no manual hold placed by Support, Sales, Finance, or Legal
   - customer is not in a protected segment, such as enterprise contract, regulated account, or strategic exception

   The system should distinguish between:

   - `overdue`
   - `at risk`
   - `pending suspension`
   - `suspended`
   - `appeal open`
   - `reinstatement pending`

3. **Design The Customer Notice Path**

   Before suspension, the customer should get a clear sequence:

   - first overdue reminder
   - warning with payment link and due date
   - final notice with suspension date
   - suspension confirmation only after action is taken

   Every notice should explain:

   - what invoice is overdue
   - what will happen
   - when it will happen
   - how to pay
   - how to appeal or contact support
   - what access, data, or services will be affected

4. **Build In An Appeal And Hold Path**

   Appeals should pause suspension automatically.

   A customer should be able to appeal because of:

   - disputed invoice
   - payment already sent
   - billing error
   - contract mismatch
   - hardship or negotiated extension
   - account ownership issue

   Internally, the appeal should create a visible case with an owner, status, due date, and decision log. While the appeal is open, the account should not be suspended unless a named owner explicitly overrides that hold.

5. **Require Human Review Before First Automation**

   For rollout, I would not start with full automatic suspension.

   Initial path:

   - system identifies eligible accounts
   - Finance/RevOps reviews the queue
   - Support/Success can add holds
   - approved accounts move to pending suspension
   - final notices are sent
   - suspension happens only after the notice window expires

   Later, if evidence supports it, low-risk accounts can move to automatic suspension under strict rules.

6. **Define Reinstatement**

   Reinstatement should be as deliberate as suspension.

   The workflow should specify:

   - what payment or approval clears suspension
   - how quickly access returns
   - whether partial payment is enough
   - who can manually reinstate
   - how customer communications are sent
   - how the audit trail records the reason

7. **Add Auditability And Safety Controls**

   Every state change should record:

   - invoice ID
   - account ID
   - previous state
   - new state
   - triggering rule
   - timestamp
   - actor, either system or human
   - notices sent
   - appeal or hold status

   Safety controls should include dry-run mode, allowlists, blocklists, rate limits, and a kill switch.

8. **Roll Out Safely**

   Rollout should move in stages:

   - policy review with Finance, Support, Success, Legal, and Engineering
   - dry run using historical overdue invoices
   - internal-only dashboard showing who would be suspended
   - manual approval pilot on a small low-risk segment
   - monitored rollout with daily review
   - gradual automation only after false positives are acceptably low

   Success metrics should include recovered payments, appeal volume, mistaken suspension rate, support burden, time to reinstate, and customer complaints.

**Stop Conditions**

I would stop rollout or keep the workflow manual if:

- invoice data is not reliable
- notices are not provably delivered
- appeal holds are not enforced
- reinstatement is slow or flaky
- ownership is unclear
- false positives appear in dry run or pilot
- legal or contract terms are unresolved

The acceptance gate before building would be a signed-off workflow spec covering ownership, eligibility, notices, appeals, audit trail, reinstatement, rollout stages, and rollback controls.
