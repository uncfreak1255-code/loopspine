**Workflow I Would Follow**

I would treat this as a high-risk plan lane because it can suspend customer accounts and affect revenue, access, and trust.

```yaml
lane: plan
goal: approved suspension workflow with clear ownership, customer notice, appeal path, rollout gates, and rollback criteria
progress: each pass must reduce ambiguity around who decides, when action happens, how customers recover, and how mistakes are prevented
budget: finite worklist with no-progress stop
proof: written workflow reviewed by billing, support, legal/compliance, product, and engineering owners
stop: success | blocked | approval-required
owner: billing operations as business owner, engineering as implementation owner
```

**Workflow Shape**

1. Define ownership

Billing owns the suspension policy and invoice state.

Support owns customer communication and appeal intake.

Engineering owns automation, access controls, audit logs, and rollback tools.

Legal/compliance reviews notice language, grace periods, and account-impact rules.

A named business owner must approve the first rollout and any policy changes.

2. Define eligibility

Only accounts with confirmed overdue invoices enter the workflow.

Before suspension is even considered, the system should verify:

- invoice is past due by the approved threshold
- payment retry attempts are complete
- customer received required notices
- no active billing dispute or appeal exists
- no internal hold, enterprise exception, or contract-specific protection applies
- account is not in a protected class such as trial, migration, incident recovery, or manual billing review

3. Customer notice sequence

The workflow should use staged notices before any suspension:

- initial overdue notice
- reminder with payment link and due date
- final warning with exact suspension date
- suspension notice only after action is taken

Each notice should say what is happening, why it matters, what the customer can do next, and how to appeal.

4. Appeal path

Customers need a clear way to stop or reverse suspension.

Appeals should create a visible case owned by support or billing. While an appeal is open, suspension should pause automatically unless a policy owner manually overrides it with a logged reason.

Appeal outcomes should be:

- payment resolved
- payment plan approved
- billing error confirmed
- dispute rejected
- extension granted
- suspension approved

Every outcome needs an owner, timestamp, reason, and customer-visible next step.

5. Suspension behavior

Suspension should be reversible and limited.

Preferred first stage: restrict non-essential actions while preserving login, billing access, data export, support contact, and payment recovery.

Avoid immediate hard lockout unless the business explicitly approves it.

The suspension action should produce an audit record showing:

- invoice IDs
- account ID
- notices sent
- eligibility checks passed
- appeal status
- actor or automation version
- rollback command/path

6. Rollout

Start with dry-run mode.

Dry run should identify accounts that would be suspended without taking action. Billing and support review the list manually.

Then move through:

- internal test accounts
- small allowlisted cohort
- low-risk customer segment
- broader rollout with daily monitoring

Each phase needs stop conditions, such as unexpected appeal volume, false positives, failed notices, support overload, or payment recovery problems.

7. Safety gates

Before automation can suspend anything, require:

- policy owner approval
- legal/compliance signoff
- support playbook ready
- billing dashboard ready
- manual override available
- rollback tested
- monitoring and alerting live
- dry-run results reviewed

8. Success and stop conditions

Success means the workflow can identify overdue accounts, notify customers, pause for appeal, suspend only eligible accounts, and reverse mistakes quickly.

Stop if:

- eligibility rules are ambiguous
- notices are not provably delivered
- appeal ownership is unclear
- rollback is untested
- contract exceptions are not modeled
- support cannot handle the volume
- legal/compliance has not approved the policy

This should not be built until the policy, ownership, appeal handling, and rollout gates are approved in writing.
