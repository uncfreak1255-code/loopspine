I would handle this as a planning and risk-design task, not an implementation task.

**Workflow**

1. **Define Ownership**
   - Billing owns invoice state and overdue rules.
   - Customer Success owns customer communication and exceptions.
   - Support owns inbound questions and appeal intake.
   - Product/Engineering owns the automation, audit trail, and safety controls.
   - Finance or Ops owns final suspension policy approval.

2. **Map Account States**
   I would define explicit states instead of jumping straight from “overdue” to “suspended”:

   - `current`
   - `invoice_due`
   - `overdue_notice_sent`
   - `at_risk_of_suspension`
   - `suspension_pending`
   - `suspended`
   - `appeal_pending`
   - `restored`
   - `exempt`

   Each transition should have a clear owner, reason, timestamp, and audit record.

3. **Set Guardrails**
   Suspension should never happen from a single raw invoice signal. I would require checks like:

   - invoice is genuinely overdue past the policy threshold
   - payment provider confirms no pending payment
   - account is not marked exempt
   - no open appeal or active support escalation
   - customer has received required notices
   - suspension window is outside sensitive periods if applicable
   - manual override exists for high-value or legally sensitive accounts

4. **Design Customer Notices**
   The workflow should include staged, plain-language communication:

   - first overdue notice
   - reminder with payment link
   - warning that access may be limited
   - final notice before suspension
   - suspension confirmation with restore steps
   - appeal instructions in every serious notice

5. **Create Appeal Path**
   Customers need a visible way to challenge or delay suspension.

   Appeal intake should capture:
   - account
   - invoice
   - reason
   - supporting notes or documents
   - requested outcome
   - owner
   - deadline

   While an appeal is open, automation should pause suspension unless policy says otherwise.

6. **Choose Suspension Behavior**
   I would avoid destructive account changes. Prefer reversible access limits:

   - block new usage or new bookings/actions
   - keep data readable where appropriate
   - preserve admin access to billing and support
   - allow payment and appeal actions
   - avoid deleting data or canceling active commitments automatically

7. **Add Auditability**
   Every automated decision should produce a readable record:

   - what invoice triggered the action
   - which policy rule matched
   - which notices were sent
   - which guardrails passed
   - who approved or overrode
   - when access changed
   - how restoration happened

8. **Safe Rollout**
   I would roll this out in phases:

   - policy review with Finance, Legal, Support, and Customer Success
   - dry-run mode only, producing “would suspend” reports
   - internal review of dry-run accounts
   - limited pilot on low-risk account segments
   - manual approval before first real suspensions
   - gradual automation with thresholds and kill switch
   - regular review of false positives, appeals, and customer impact

9. **Acceptance Gates Before Building**
   Before implementation, I would want written answers for:

   - exact overdue thresholds
   - who can exempt an account
   - who can approve suspension
   - what pauses automation
   - what restores access
   - what customer data remains accessible
   - what the appeal SLA is
   - what metrics define a safe rollout

The important design principle is that billing automation can recommend or prepare suspension, but actual suspension should be reversible, explainable, appealable, and guarded by clear ownership.
