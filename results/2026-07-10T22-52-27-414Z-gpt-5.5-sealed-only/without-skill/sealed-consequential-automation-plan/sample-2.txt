I’d treat this as a policy-sensitive product workflow, not just an automation task. The goal is to design a suspension process that is fair, auditable, reversible, and hard to trigger accidentally.

**Workflow**

1. **Define Ownership**
   
   Assign one clear owner for each stage:
   
   - Billing owns invoice status, payment reminders, and overdue thresholds.
   - Customer Success owns customer communication and escalation context.
   - Support owns appeal intake and customer-facing explanations.
   - Finance or Ops owns final suspension approval policy.
   - Engineering owns implementation only after the policy and controls are approved.

   I would avoid letting an unattended job be the sole decision-maker for suspension.

2. **Map The Account States**
   
   Define explicit states before automation:
   
   - `current`
   - `payment_due`
   - `overdue_warning`
   - `final_notice`
   - `pending_suspension`
   - `suspended`
   - `appeal_pending`
   - `restored`

   Each transition should have a documented trigger, owner, timestamp, and customer-visible message.

3. **Set Suspension Criteria**
   
   Suspension should require more than “invoice overdue.”
   
   Example gates:
   
   - Invoice is overdue by a defined number of days.
   - Multiple reminders were sent.
   - No active payment dispute exists.
   - No approved payment plan exists.
   - Account is not marked exempt by Finance/Ops.
   - Final notice period has elapsed.
   - Suspension action is approved or eligible under policy.

4. **Create The Appeal Path**
   
   Before suspension, every final notice should include a clear appeal option.

   The appeal process should define:
   
   - How customers appeal.
   - Who reviews appeals.
   - Expected response time.
   - What pauses suspension.
   - What evidence or context is needed.
   - How restoration happens if the appeal succeeds.

   While an appeal is pending, the account should move to `appeal_pending`, not `suspended`.

5. **Design Human Review For Risky Cases**
   
   I would require manual review for:
   
   - Large accounts.
   - Recently renewed customers.
   - Accounts with open support escalations.
   - Customers with payment processor errors.
   - Accounts with partial payments.
   - First-time overdue customers.
   - Any account near a configurable revenue or usage threshold.

6. **Build Auditability Into The Plan**
   
   Every decision should produce a record:
   
   - Invoice IDs involved.
   - Reminder timestamps.
   - Policy version used.
   - Account state before and after.
   - Actor or job that initiated the transition.
   - Whether a human approved it.
   - Customer messages sent.
   - Appeal status.

   This matters because suspension is customer-impacting and may become a support, legal, or revenue issue.

7. **Safe Rollout Plan**
   
   I would roll it out in phases:
   
   - Phase 1: reporting only, no customer impact.
   - Phase 2: internal review queue only.
   - Phase 3: send warnings, but do not suspend.
   - Phase 4: suspend a small low-risk cohort with manual approval.
   - Phase 5: expand with monitoring and rollback controls.

   The first production version should default to “flag for review,” not “suspend automatically.”

8. **Rollback And Restoration**
   
   Before any suspension exists, restoration must be designed.

   Restoration should happen when:
   
   - Payment clears.
   - An appeal is approved.
   - An operator grants an exception.
   - A mistaken suspension is detected.

   Restoration should be fast, logged, and customer-visible.

9. **Acceptance Gate Before Building**
   
   I would not implement until these are approved:
   
   - Written policy for overdue thresholds and exemptions.
   - State transition diagram.
   - Owner map.
   - Appeal process.
   - Customer message templates.
   - Audit requirements.
   - Rollout and rollback plan.
   - Test cases for edge conditions.

The workflow I’d follow is: define the policy and states first, review the customer-risk cases, design the appeal and restoration paths, then build the smallest system that can safely flag accounts before any automatic suspension is allowed.
