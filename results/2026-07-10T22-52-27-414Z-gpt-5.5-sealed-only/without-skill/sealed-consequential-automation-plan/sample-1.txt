I would treat this as a planning/spec workflow, not an implementation task.

**Workflow**

1. **Define Ownership**
   Establish who owns each decision:
   - Billing owns invoice status and payment records.
   - Customer Success owns customer communication and relationship exceptions.
   - Support owns inbound questions and appeal intake.
   - Product/Engineering owns the account state change mechanism.
   - Legal/Compliance reviews suspension language, grace periods, and edge cases.

2. **Map The Account Lifecycle**
   Define the exact states before building anything:
   - `active`
   - `payment_overdue`
   - `at_risk_of_suspension`
   - `suspended_limited_access`
   - `suspended_full`
   - `reactivated`
   - `appeal_pending`

   Each state should have a clear trigger, owner, customer-facing message, and reversal path.

3. **Set Suspension Rules**
   Write the policy in plain terms first:
   - How many days overdue before warning.
   - How many reminders are sent.
   - Which customers are exempt or require manual review.
   - Whether suspension is partial or full.
   - What happens to data, active users, integrations, and API access.
   - What payment or approval reactivates the account.

4. **Add Human Review Before Harmful Actions**
   I would not allow automatic suspension at first. The first version should produce a review queue for Billing or Customer Success.

   The system can recommend suspension, but a named owner approves it until the policy has proven reliable.

5. **Create The Appeal Path**
   Customers need a clear way to dispute or delay suspension:
   - Appeal link in every overdue notice.
   - Support queue or form tagged as billing appeal.
   - Temporary hold state while appeal is reviewed.
   - SLA for response.
   - Clear outcomes: approved extension, denied, more information needed, payment plan, or reactivation.

6. **Design Customer Communications**
   Draft messages for each stage:
   - First overdue notice.
   - Final warning.
   - Suspension notice.
   - Appeal received.
   - Appeal decision.
   - Reactivation confirmation.

   These should say what happened, why it matters, what the customer can do next, and who to contact.

7. **Define Safety Controls**
   Before rollout, require:
   - Audit log for every state change.
   - Manual override with reason.
   - Exemption list.
   - Dry-run mode.
   - Internal notifications before suspension.
   - Rate limits on how many accounts can be suspended per day.
   - Rollback path to restore access quickly.

8. **Roll Out Gradually**
   I would phase this in:
   - Phase 1: reporting only, no customer impact.
   - Phase 2: internal review queue.
   - Phase 3: send warnings automatically, still manual suspension.
   - Phase 4: limited automatic suspension for low-risk segments.
   - Phase 5: broader automation after reviewing incidents, appeals, and false positives.

9. **Measure And Review**
   Track:
   - Number of overdue accounts.
   - Warnings sent.
   - Suspensions recommended.
   - Suspensions approved.
   - Appeals opened and outcomes.
   - False positives.
   - Reactivation time.
   - Customer complaints or escalations.

10. **Acceptance Gate Before Build**
   I would not build until there is a signed-off workflow document covering:
   - Ownership matrix.
   - Account states and transitions.
   - Customer communication drafts.
   - Appeal handling process.
   - Rollout phases.
   - Audit and rollback requirements.

The main principle: billing automation can recommend and route decisions early, but anything that removes customer access needs ownership, visibility, appeal, and rollback before it becomes automatic.
